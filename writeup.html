How Did the Community Build the Hyper-Local Air Monitoring Network?

Background

Since 2007, community members from Cheverly, Fairmount Heights, Seat Pleasant, Cedar Heights, and adjoining neighborhoods have been concerned about health risks from heavy diesel traffic and nearby industries. These include diesel bus maintenance and dispatch facilities, food and package warehouse distribution facilities, concrete batching and aggregate industries, cremation facilities, wastewater treatment facilities, and metal shredding and recycling operations.

Beginning in 2018, the Center for Community Engagement, Environmental Justice and Health (CEEJH) at the University of Maryland established a PurpleAir sensor network in collaboration with Cheverly and neighboring communities. In 2021, the Maryland Department of the Environment used the hyper-local sensor network as part of the Cheverly Targeted Inspection Initiative to help identify concerns and inspect air permit compliance. Community members continue to report possible pollution events to MDE.

For years, residents have also reported explosions and grinding vibrations, often at night, as well as the loss of natural green space from proposed and completed industrial development. Community members are concerned that these conditions can worsen air pollution, noise, vibration, and flooding.

In 2024, five C-12 black carbon monitors were added to the network. In 2025, the network expanded with QuantAQ MODULAIR instruments and SPod volatile organic compound instruments. The QuantAQ instruments provide measurements used in the dashboard for particulate matter, ozone, carbon monoxide, and nitrogen dioxide. Additional particle sampling has also been conducted to improve understanding of fine particles in these neighborhoods.

Why Did EJAT Develop the CE-AQI?

The reason behind the CE-AQI

After years of frustration over breathing polluted air while regional tools often described local air quality as good, the Environmental Justice Action Team (EJAT) began developing a different way to communicate risk. Traditional AQI tools consider a limited group of criteria pollutants and may not reflect some of the highest-risk pollutants measured by the community's hyper-local network.

After discussions with public health and environmental experts, EJAT developed the CE-AQI to communicate the combined impact of multiple criteria and toxic air pollutants measured in and around the community.

A pilot effort

The CE-AQI is a pilot method. It is not an official EPA or MDE index, and it is expected to improve as EJAT receives additional feedback, data, and scientific review. The purpose of the pilot is to make high-priority pollutants visible rather than leaving them out of the public discussion simply because they are not included in the traditional AQI.

Useful feedback

EJAT welcomes feedback that helps improve the method, data quality, health communication, and scientific basis of the dashboard. The central question is how to responsibly communicate the pollutants already being measured while research on cumulative exposure continues.

Important: CE-AQI values use the familiar AQI color categories for communication, but CE-AQI is an experimental community index. It should not be treated as an official regulatory determination or as medical advice.

How Is the Current CE-AQI Calculated?

The current dashboard uses the CE-AQI pilot framework, with implementation details added so that every displayed station can receive a complete pollutant packet.

Step 1: Assemble the pollutant packet

Each displayed station includes every usable pollutant variable available to the network. When a station does not measure a pollutant directly, the dashboard uses the closest usable QuantAQ or C-12 sensor. This is why several nearby stations can show similar CE-AQI values.

  • PM2.5: The dashboard uses the closest usable QuantAQ 24-hour value when available. PurpleAir serves as the fallback source.
  • PM10: QuantAQ 24-hour value.
  • Ozone: QuantAQ highest 8-hour value.
  • Carbon monoxide: QuantAQ highest 8-hour value.
  • Nitrogen dioxide: QuantAQ highest 1-hour value.
  • Black carbon: C-12 data are compared with the pilot non-cancer reference of 600 ng/m³.
  • Diesel particulate matter: Black carbon is multiplied by 1.25, then compared with the 330 ng/m³ pilot health-protection benchmark selected for this version.

Step 2: Identify the Base Value

The pollutant with the highest valid pollutant-specific AQI becomes the Base Value. A value borrowed from the closest supporting sensor can become the base when it is the highest pollutant risk assigned to that location.

Step 3: Calculate the Adders

The pollutant used as the Base Value does not receive an adder. Each other pollutant receives an adder based on its own pollutant-specific AQI:

  • AQI 80 to 99: add 1% of the Base Value.
  • AQI 100 to 119: add 2% of the Base Value.
  • AQI 120 or higher: add 3% of the Base Value.
  • AQI below 80: included in the breakdown, but no adder is applied.

Step 4: Calculate the Final CE-AQI

The dashboard adds the Base Value and all applicable adders. The final result determines the station's CE-AQI number, marker color, health category, and displayed main driver.

Current limitations: The nearest-sensor method is an estimate, not a direct measurement of every pollutant at every station. The C-12 live concentration and the stored average used in the CE-AQI may represent different time periods. Missing, delayed, or stale data can also affect which pollutants are available.

Questions About the Dashboard

Why do several stations have similar CE-AQI values?

Many stations measure only part of the pollutant packet. The dashboard fills missing variables with the closest usable QuantAQ or C-12 sensor. Stations near the same supporting sensors may therefore share several pollutant values and receive similar scores.

Why can the CE-AQI be higher than the regular AQI?

The regular AQI reports the highest value among a limited group of criteria pollutants. The CE-AQI also includes black carbon and diesel particulate matter, then adds small cumulative-exposure adders for other elevated pollutants. The two indexes are related communication tools, but they are not the same calculation.

What does “Main driver” mean?

The main driver is the pollutant with the highest pollutant-specific AQI in that station's packet. It becomes the Base Value used in the CE-AQI calculation.

What does “Latest detected” mean?

It is the timestamp associated with the latest displayed station reading. Some CE-AQI components use rolling or stored averages, so the latest detected time does not mean every pollutant in the packet was measured at that exact minute.

Why does the page sometimes take a few seconds to load?

The dashboard combines PurpleAir, QuantAQ, C-12, database averages, map markers, and recent trend data. Requests are intentionally queued and cached so the database is not overloaded. The map may appear before all station details and trend data finish loading.

Are stale readings used?

The dashboard skips a C-12 live reading when it is more than three hours old. A stored C-12 average may still be used in the CE-AQI calculation when historical data are available. The station panel shows the latest detected time so users can judge how current the displayed concentration is.

Can I use this dashboard for medical or regulatory decisions?

No. This is an experimental community-awareness tool. For official air-quality information, regulatory decisions, or personal medical guidance, use the appropriate government agency or health professional.

AQI Categories and Cautionary Statements

The dashboard uses the familiar AQI category colors to communicate the CE-AQI result.

AQI Category Index Value Description of Air Quality
Good (Green) 0 to 50 Air quality is satisfactory, and air pollution poses little or no risk.
Moderate (Yellow) 51 to 100 Air quality is acceptable. However, there may be a risk for some people, particularly those who are unusually sensitive to air pollution.
Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups (Orange) 101 to 150 Members of sensitive groups may experience health effects. The general public is less likely to be affected.
Unhealthy (Red) 151 to 200 Some members of the general public may experience health effects. Members of sensitive groups may experience more serious health effects.
Very Unhealthy (Purple) 201 to 300 Health alert: The risk of health effects is increased for everyone.
Hazardous (Maroon) 301 and higher Health warning of emergency conditions: Everyone is more likely to be affected.

Who Is Taking Action to Clean the Air?

Since 2018, the Cheverly community has worked with the University of Maryland's CEEJH and the Maryland Department of the Environment to address air pollution and other environmental justice concerns associated with nearby stationary and mobile pollution sources.

How can I help?

Community reports can help MDE identify possible violations and determine when and where a problem is occurring. When it is safe to do so, record the location, date, time, odor or visible emissions, how long the event lasted, and any other useful details. Photos or video can also help when they can be collected safely and legally.
